We Vibe-Coded a World Cup Tracker (And Then It Got Personal)

July 8, 2026  ·  Jack Blair  ·  Happy Jack Media

Let me be upfront about something: I am not a football journalist. I am not traveling to any of the 16 host cities. I am not on the ground in Dallas or New York or Los Angeles filing match reports. I'm a filmmaker sitting at my desk, increasingly obsessed with a tournament I've been watching from my couch — and at some point I decided the best way to channel that energy was to build something.

So I did. With a lot of help from AI.

The Tracker Was Born Out of Pure Excitement

When the 2026 World Cup draw happened and Scotland's name came out — Scotland, who hadn't been to a World Cup since France '98 — something clicked. This tournament suddenly felt personal. The family was fired up. Group chats were buzzing. And I realized pretty quickly that half the people I was going to be watching with had no idea what a group stage was, what "goal difference" meant, or why a draw could sometimes feel like a win.

That's where the idea for the tracker came from. Not a tool for the diehards — they've got ESPN and BBC Sport. This was for the family members watching their first World Cup, the ones asking "wait, so why did they just celebrate a tie?" I wanted something simple, clean, and genuinely useful for people who were new to all of this.

What Vibe-Coding Actually Looks Like

I've been using AI tools in my production work for a while now, but this project was the first time I leaned into what people are calling "vibe-coding" — basically describing what you want, iterating rapidly, and letting the AI handle the heavy lifting on the code while you focus on the design decisions and the user experience.

The process went something like this: I'd describe a feature — "I want a live scoreboard that updates automatically and shows which teams are advancing" — and Claude would generate the HTML, CSS, and JavaScript. I'd look at it in the browser, think about how my non-soccer-literate family would interpret it, and then describe the next iteration. "Can we make the advancement status more obvious? Can we add a little explainer tooltip on what 'GD' means?"

What I genuinely didn't expect was how quickly it moved. The first working prototype — a live scoreboard pulling real match data, formatted cleanly, readable on a phone — came together in an afternoon. That would have taken me weeks to build from scratch, if I even could have built it at all. The iteration cycle was fast enough that I could watch a match, notice something confusing about the display, and fix it before the next game kicked off.

"The iteration cycle was fast enough that I could watch a match, notice something confusing about the display, and fix it before the next game kicked off."

Over the course of the group stage, the tracker evolved from a basic scoreboard into something with live standings, match schedules, group advancement indicators, and contextual notes to help newcomers understand what they were looking at. It became its own little project — one I'm genuinely proud of, even if it started as a weekend experiment.

You can still check it out at worldcup.happyjack.media.

Scotland. Oh, Scotland.

I need to talk about this because it genuinely hurt.

Scotland qualifying for this World Cup was already a miracle worth celebrating — their first tournament since France 1998, twenty-eight years of hurt finally ending. The family was electric. There was real belief that this expanded 48-team format gave Scotland a genuine path through the group stage. All they had to do was avoid finishing bottom.

They did not avoid finishing bottom.

A 3-0 loss to Brazil in their final group match — Vinícius Jr. scoring twice, Matheus Cunha adding a third — was the kind of scoreline that doesn't tell the whole story, but also kind of does. Scotland fought. They competed. But Brazil were in a different league, and that was that. Even the expanded format's grace of qualifying eight third-place teams couldn't save them. Scotland were out, Steve Clarke resigned, and we were left refreshing the group table hoping the math would somehow change.

It did not change.

This is football. This is Scotland. And yet somehow it still stings every time.

The USA: A Host Nation Moment That Wasn't

Look — I wanted the US to do well. When you're hosting a World Cup for the first time in three decades, on home soil, with the whole country suddenly paying attention, you want the home side to deliver something to remember. A quarterfinal run. A giant-killing. Something to put on the poster.

Instead: Belgium 4, USA 1. In Seattle. In the Round of 16.

Malik Tillman equalized with a brilliant free kick and for about ten minutes it felt like maybe, maybe this was the moment. Then De Ketelaere scored. Then Vanaken. Then Lukaku in stoppage time, just to really drive the point home. The US were outclassed in a way that was difficult to watch, and all three host nations — the US, Canada, and Mexico — went home before the quarterfinals. The entire co-hosting experiment knocked out in the same round.

My son Cole and I watched that match in a pub in New York City. The atmosphere in the first half — when Malik Tillman's free kick went in and it felt like anything was possible — was genuinely electric. People were on their feet. The pub was loud. We looked at each other like, okay, this might actually happen. And then it didn't. Thoroughly didn't. We stayed until the final whistle, shook our heads, and went to find somewhere to eat and talk about what we'd just witnessed.

It's not a disaster for American soccer — the sport is genuinely growing here and the tournament itself has been a massive success. But as a fan sitting in that pub, watching that 4-1 scoreline finalize in real time, it was a deflating afternoon.

But Then There's Mbappe

And this is where the tournament has given us something to actually look forward to.

Kylian Mbappé has been extraordinary. Seven goals in the tournament. Tied at the top of the Golden Boot table. A penalty against Paraguay in the Round of 16 that sent France through, and now a quarterfinal date with Morocco — a rematch of the 2022 semifinal — at Foxborough on Thursday.

We are fully on the France train. There's something about watching Mbappe at a World Cup that makes even the most casual observer lean forward. He's chasing Messi's all-time scoring record with the kind of focus and inevitability that makes you feel like you're watching history happen in real time. Ousmane Dembele has been electric too — a hat-trick against Norway in the groups felt like a statement. France are dangerous in a way that's genuinely exciting to follow.

After Scotland's exit and the USA's early goodbye, this is where our hope lives. Allez les Bleus.

The Family Watching Experience

Some World Cups you watch alone, on a laptop, in a quiet room. This one has been something else entirely.

We actually started getting ready before the first match kicked off. My son and I built the official Mbappe Lego set together — yes, there is a Lego Mbappe set, complete with the Arc de Triomphe, a tiny #10 minifig, and the Ballon d'Or trophy in miniature — and it's been sitting on the shelf in the living room ever since. Our little pre-tournament ritual. Our declaration of intent.

The tracker has genuinely become the center of the family group chat. I built it for exactly this reason — for the family members who show up excited but aren't totally sure what a group stage is, or why a 0-0 draw can sometimes be cause for relief rather than disappointment. Watching that actually work in real time has been more satisfying than I expected. People checking the standings on their phones and immediately understanding where their team is. Fewer "wait, so are they out?" messages. The tracker doing its job.

My daughter Avy has been all in from day one. She's been playing herself this spring — CR Sports, Spring 2026 — and there's something that clicks once you've actually been on a pitch. She's been asking tactical questions I'm not entirely qualified to answer, and I've been absolutely loving the attempt.

For a lot of us in the family, this is the first World Cup we've really followed closely — not just caught a final by accident, but actually tracked, watched, argued about. That's the thing about a tournament this size. It creates a shared calendar. Everyone knows when the big matches are. The group chat is active in a way it usually isn't. And even when your team goes home early (Scotland, I'm looking at you), the tournament keeps going and there's always something new to discuss.
